Olympics Accessibility

How meritocratic are the Games really?

Introduction

The Olympic Games are one of the most viewed sporting events around the world. More than 200 states participate across 35 sports in more than 400 events. In the 2016 Rio Summer Olympics 3.6 Billion people tuned in to the 2016 Rio Summer Olympics while 1.9 billion watched the 2018 PyeongChang Winter Games. That means over half of the world watches the Summer Olympics, and this is without considering the increasing number of people who watch on streaming services instead of TV! But even if the Olympics are easily accessible to interested viewers, how accessible are the Games to athletes? More specifically, what does it take to be an Olympic athlete? Let’s take a look.

How many athletes are from your home state?

We can also think about inclusivity and access in terms of geographic representation. Let’s first take a look at the U.S. Where do U.S. Olympic athletes come from?
The colors on the map show the median household incomes of US counties and each red dot indicates the hometown of a US Olympic athlete. We see that, on the whole, athletes are more likely to come from areas with higher median household incomes. This is interesting…might income be an indicator for one’s potential to become an olympic athlete?

How does household income of a hometown affect number of athletes sent to the Olympics?

Athletes are more likely to come from households with higher income levels than the median household income. Perhaps participating in the Olympics is not just determined by natural talent, but by the ability to access resources that hone, train, and build that talent. A meritocracy?

So what does this all say about the accessibility of the Olympic Games? Olympic sports are only biased in terms of the physical traits they favor, they are also biased in terms of the resources that athletes and countries have access to. Being of a particular physique is an advantage but so is being of a particular income background. The Olympic Games might not be the truly meritocratic competition we think it is.
